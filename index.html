Pupil & Neurological Awareness

Cutman Checklist

Pupil Observation

  • Pupils should be equal in size
  • Pupils should react normally to light
  • Sudden dilation after a heavy shot is a warning sign
  • Unequal pupils (anisocoria) are a serious red flag
  • Sluggish or non-reactive pupils indicate possible neurological distress
  • Worsening pupil response round-to-round must be taken seriously

Possible Causes of Abnormal Pupils

  • Concussion
  • Raised intracranial pressure / brain trauma
  • Severe neurological shock

Normal Reasons Pupils May Dilate

  • Adrenaline / fight-or-flight response
  • Fatigue
  • Stress and exertion
  • Lighting changes between ring and corner

Cutman Responsibilities

  • Observe and report, not diagnose
  • Monitor symmetry and reactivity, not size alone
  • Immediately inform the referee or ringside doctor if concerned

Quick Corner Safety Check

  • Eyes tracking and focused
  • Pupils equal and responsive
  • Speech clear
  • Balance steady
  • Fighter follows instructions
  • Unequal or non-reactive pupils require immediate escalation

Common Hand Injuries in Boxing

A practical reference list for boxers, coaches, and corners

  • Boxer's fracture – fracture of the 5th metacarpal (little-finger knuckle)
  • Metacarpal fractures (2nd–4th) – breaks to the knuckle bones from impact
  • Metacarpal stress fractures – overuse injuries from high training volume
  • Knuckle contusions – bruising and soft-tissue damage over the knuckles
  • Sagittal band rupture (Boxer's knuckle) – tendon stabiliser injury at the knuckle
  • Tendon strains or tears – flexor or extensor tendon damage
  • Wrist sprains – ligament injury due to poor alignment or wrapping
  • Carpal bone fractures – commonly scaphoid fractures near the thumb
  • Thumb injuries – sprains, ligament tears, or fractures
  • Ligament damage – instability of finger or thumb joints
  • Joint inflammation (synovitis) – swelling and stiffness from repetitive trauma
  • Nerve compression or irritation – numbness or tingling in fingers
  • Skin splits and lacerations – cuts over knuckles risking infection

Prevention Note

Early reporting, proper hand wrapping, suitable gloves, and load management reduce long-term damage.

The Importance of 4–4.5 Metre Hand Wraps

Essential Boxing Equipment

In proper boxing training and competition, 4–4.5 metre hand wraps are essential equipment. They are designed to protect the hands, wrists, and thumbs while improving punching safety and longevity.

Key Benefits

  • Knuckle protection: The length allows multiple layers over the knuckles, reducing fractures, splits, and long-term damage
  • Wrist stability: Longer wraps secure the wrist using figure-eight techniques, helping keep it straight on impact
  • Thumb and metacarpal support: Correct wrapping stabilises the thumb and protects common fracture points in the hand
  • Impact distribution: Wraps fill the glove correctly so force is spread across the hand rather than concentrated on one knuckle
  • Professional standard: 4–4.5 metre wraps are the accepted standard in serious amateur and professional boxing gyms
4m
Minimum Length
4.5m
Preferred Length

Hand wraps provide structural support, effectively creating a protective cast around the hand. They are a fundamental part of safe and effective boxing training, not an optional extra.

FIT2FIGHT 08

Unseen Issues & Hematoma

Boxing Corner Safety Guide

Unseen Issues (Not Visible but High Risk)

  • Brain injury can occur without visible cuts or swelling
  • Concussion signs include delayed reactions, poor balance, confusion, or glazed eyes
  • Repeated head trauma increases risk of second impact syndrome
  • Internal brain bleeding can develop hours after the bout
  • Post-fight warning signs: worsening headache, nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, slurred speech
  • Sustained punishment causes cumulative neurological damage
  • Fighters may insist on continuing despite impairment
  • Corner must intervene before damage becomes irreversible

Hematoma (Visible Injury)

  • Blood pooling under the skin caused by damaged blood vessels
  • Common areas: around the eye, cheekbone, brow ridge, ear
  • Swelling increases pressure and tissue damage
  • Can impair vision and force a medical stoppage
  • Swollen skin splits more easily, worsening cuts
  • Rapid expansion requires close monitoring
  • Poor management increases risk of doctor or referee stoppage

Corner Responsibility

  • Protect the fighter from unnecessary damage
  • Assess neurological function, not just visible injuries
  • Manage swelling immediately and effectively
  • Recognise when continuation is unsafe
  • Early stoppage prevents long-term injury
  • Fighter safety always outweighs winning the bout
